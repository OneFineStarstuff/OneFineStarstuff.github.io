Analytical Rationale & Methodological Framework

This report synthesises three distinct methodological traditions: (1) Technology governance theory — applying Collingridge’s dilemma to argue for adaptive governance structures rather than premature regulatory lock-in. (2) Enterprise risk management — extending COSO ERM and ISO 31000 to AGI-specific risk categories including capability jumps, alignment failures, economic disruption, and regulatory discontinuity. (3) International relations theory — drawing on regime theory and epistemic community frameworks to assess multilateral AGI governance feasibility analogous to IAEA (nuclear), ICAO (aviation), and FSB (financial stability).

Capability calibration: Timeline projections derived from published scaling laws (Hoffmann et al. 2022, Kaplan et al. 2020), Epoch AI 2025 compute analysis, and observed frontier benchmarks — ARC-AGI-2 (28.9%), FrontierMath (43.2%), SWE-bench Verified (72.7%). Economic modelling: McKinsey Global Institute 2025 revision ($13.2–$22.1T GDP impact), Goldman Sachs (Briggs & Kodnani 2024), IMF 2024 analyses. Investment calibration: Derived from comparable enterprise governance programmes (SOX: $2–5M, GDPR: $1.5–4M, Cybersecurity: $6.2M annual), adjusted for AGI-specific complexities.

This framework proposes six governance pillars — Capability Monitoring, Alignment Assurance, Economic Preparedness, Regulatory Readiness, Organisational Transformation, and International Engagement — with a recommended investment of $4.8 million over 24 months. Frameworks cited: NIST AI RMF 1.0, ISO 42001:2023, EU AI Act, OECD AI Principles, Bletchley Declaration, Seoul Commitments.

Frameworks Cited

The convergence of scaling laws, architectural innovation, and compute availability places artificial general intelligence — systems matching or exceeding human-level performance across virtually all economically valuable cognitive tasks — within a credible planning horizon of 5 to 15 years (central estimate: 2031–2036). This represents a material strategic risk demanding board-level governance attention today, not upon arrival.

The implications are tripartite. Economic transformation: McKinsey’s 2025 revision estimates $13.2–$22.1 trillion in annual global GDP impact from advanced AI by 2035, with 60–70% of current job activities automatable. Regulatory discontinuity: the EU AI Act establishes the first binding GPAI framework with systemic-risk designation at 1025 FLOP; AGI-class systems will trigger the most stringent tier across multiple jurisdictions. Existential and reputational risk: misaligned AGI-class systems pose unbounded liability exposure for early deployers without governance frameworks.

This report proposes a six-pillar governance framework with $4.8M over 24 months, designed to be adaptive: controls intensify automatically as capability milestones are reached. The Board is asked to approve the framework charter, fund Phase 1, and establish a quarterly AGI Preparedness Review as a standing agenda item.
